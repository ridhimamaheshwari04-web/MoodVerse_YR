Faded Afternoon
The light falls gold through lace curtains,
soft as a photograph of something
that has not yet become memory.

I watch the dust move in afternoon air
and think: this, too, will be something
someone misses.
— Anon., c. 1952
What Remains
The chair still holds the shape of her.
The cup still wears the ring she left.
Time is not the thief they say it is —
it is the keeper of small things.
— Anon., c. 1967
The Last Telegram
Twelve words. Stop.
They cost a fortune then,
those measured sentences.

We learned to love
inside economy —
arriving safe. Stop. Missing you. Stop.

Now words cost nothing
and we say so little.
— Anon., c. 1943
Sunday Market
The cobblestones smell of rain and bread.
A woman in a yellow coat
touches the peaches — does not buy.

I watch her from the flower stall.
She is thinking of someone.
We all are, on Sunday mornings.
— Anon., c. 1958
The Gramophone Evening
Father wound the gramophone
with the patience of a man
who believed music was prayer.

The needle found the groove.
The room filled like a church.
We did not speak — we understood
that some silences are full.
— Anon., c. 1931
Old Ink
There is something about a word
written in ink —
it commits. It cannot be undone.

The writer knew this
and chose the word carefully.
We have forgotten how to be careful.
— Anon., c. 1944
Winter in the Parlour
Snow at the window.
The fire. The book.
The dog asleep against the warmth.

This was a whole life,
once.
Perhaps it still is.
— Anon., c. 1939
